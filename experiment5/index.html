Description

In this experiment, I am trying to create a generator that reads an image
and tries to match the overall composition of it using primitive shapes and
colors. I used ellipses and rectangles because at the end of the day, you can
adapt any shape using sharp lines and arcs. The theme of this experiment, in my eyes,
is beauty in organized chaos as well as a testament to human pattern recognition.

Technical

The genereator creates primitives in a grid fashion then averages the colors
of all the pixels in the area it covers. Once that's done, it adds a slight bit
of error to the coordinates of the shape before pushing it to an object container.
Finally, it mutates the design by randomizing the coordinates and dimensions of each
primitive within a boundary through a gaussian blur function. The higher the slider factor
the more drastic the mutations are. Finally, the images are evaluated and given a similarity
score and are then recorded. Best results are achieved when mutations start big and gradually
decrease.

Reflection

Actually building this thing was a lot more difficult than I anticipated, primarily because
the example project on Professor Wes Modes portfolio was all based around black and white images.
I wanted to integrate color into mine to stand out which made the process a lot harder than it probably
should have been. Luckily thanks to Raven Cruz and Jackie Sanchez posting their solution to that
problem on the discord, I was able to debug my own and put my own spin on it by taking every pixel,
averaging out the color and using it as a fill background for each mutation.

I thought that experimenting with the images and p5jsâ€™ loadpixel() index was a lot more fun than I
anticipated. Even though it was a struggle at first, it made me realize that I enjoy overcoming
challenges that are hard under the hood but simple on the surface. But I think the part that really
shined during this experiment was actually getting to play around with the final product being part
of the requirements. At the end of the day, I felt this experiment was a pretty good one.
